Bat Re-Identifiation

Machine Learning for Bat Research and Conservation

The In-Flight Experiment and Dataset

The automated in-flight setup consists of three main components (see Figure below). The bat is attracted to an artificial blossomm containing a photoelectric sensor, which triggers when the bat inserts its tongue to access the provided sugar water. During the approach, the bat is recorded from above by a high-speed monochrome camera (238 frames per second), equipped with a varifocal (2.8–10 mm), infrared-corrected lens suitable for medium close range. Simultaneously, the bat is illuminated from below by an infrared light source designed to provide evenly diffused illumination, resulting in a bright, uniform background. We use infrared light with a wavelength of 850 nm, which is less disturbing to bats than visible light and therefore well-suited for behavioural observation and imaging. A microcontroller coordinates all active components, transmitting timestamps and sequential identifiers for each approach to a connected computer via a serial interface. The captured images are transferred to the same system. For each approach, the system records 140 consecutive frames, typically covering five to seven complete wingbeat cycles.

Re-Identification and Gait-/Motion-Recognition

Using the in-flight recordings, we evaluate a range of state-of-the-art gait and re-identification architectures that operate purely on motion information: silhouettes, poses, or short image sequences without any visible colour pattern. Despite this very challenging setting, our preliminary experiments already achieve up to 95 % mTop-1 accuracy, meaning that most approaches are assigned to the correct individual at the first rank.

These results are still preliminary and may change as we extend the dataset and refine our models. Nevertheless, they provide strong evidence that bats carry highly individual “motion signatures” in their wingbeat patterns and flight dynamics. In other words, we can recognise individuals purely from how they move, which opens up exciting possibilities for non-invasive monitoring and long-term behavioural studies in the wild and in experimental setups.
